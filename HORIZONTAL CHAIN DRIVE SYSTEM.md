HORIZONTAL CHAIN DRIVE SYSTEM

A reoriented roller-chain transmission that routes linear power around
corners using simple guide rollers, with modular gearbox junctions to
change bend planes for full 3D routing.

Author: Eric Don McElroy

Date: 2026-06-08

Contact: eric.mcelroy@gmail.com, https://substack.com/@eskamick

Abstract

The Horizontal Chain Drive System inverts the conventional orientation
of a standard roller chain so that the pins are vertical and the chain
lies flat. This shifts the chain's natural bending plane from vertical
to horizontal, allowing the drive to turn corners using only inexpensive
guide rollers instead of additional sprockets or gearboxes. Support
rollers beneath the chain counteract gravity sag. A single right‑angle
bevel‑gear set connects the drive motor anywhere along the path. Where
the bend plane must change---turning upward into a ceiling or downward
into a floor drive---a compact gearbox transitions to a new chain
segment oriented for the required plane. The result is a cheap, modular,
three‑dimensionally routable linear drive system using standard
components. This disclosure places the reoriented chain, guide‑roller
cornering, and modular plane‑change architecture into the public domain.

\-\--

Problem

Conventional roller‑chain drives for sliding gates, industrial doors,
and linear conveyance require the motor and drive sprocket to sit in a
straight line with the load path. The chain bends only in one
plane---vertically when mounted in the standard orientation. Turning a
corner demands an additional driven sprocket, a second motor, or a
complex transfer case, each adding cost, maintenance, and alignment
sensitivity. Routing a chain drive around a building corner, into a
cabinet, or through a confined space forces compromises in motor
placement and chain path that often require custom fabrication. No
simple, standardized method exists to route a single chain loop through
horizontal turns using only passive guide elements.

\-\--

Concept

The Horizontal Chain Drive System begins with a single geometric
insight: a standard roller chain bends only in the plane perpendicular
to its pins. In the conventional gate‑drive orientation, pins are
horizontal and the chain bends vertically---useless for navigating
around a corner. By flipping the entire system 90° so the pins are
vertical and the chain plates run parallel to the ground, the chain's
natural bending plane becomes horizontal. It can now wrap around a
simple vertical‑axis guide roller and change direction in the same plane
without any toothed sprocket at the corner.

Core Components

· Reoriented chain. Standard ANSI roller chain (e.g., #40, #50) mounted
with pins vertical. The chain lies flat, supported from beneath.

· Support rollers. A series of fixed, free‑spinning rollers (flanged or
grooved) positioned under the chain at regular intervals. These carry
the chain's weight and prevent gravity‑induced sag, exactly as a
conveyor belt idler supports the belt.

· Guide rollers at corners. Where the chain must turn in the horizontal
plane, two vertical‑axis guide rollers are placed---one for the outgoing
run, one for the return run. The chain wraps around these smooth
rollers, which act as passive pivot points. No teeth engage; the chain
bends naturally around the roller radius. The roller diameter is chosen
to match the chain's minimum bend radius.

· Drive interface. A single right‑angle bevel‑gear set (or worm‑gear
reducer) connects the motor's horizontal output shaft to a vertical
drive sprocket. This sprocket can be placed anywhere along the chain
path---at the end, in the middle, or tucked inside a housing. The motor
is no longer forced to sit at the end of the track.

Changing the Bend Plane (3D Routing)

If the chain must change planes---for example, rising vertically up a
wall or dropping into a floor trench---the system does not attempt to
make a single chain bend in two axes. Instead, a compact right‑angle
gearbox is inserted at the transition point. The outgoing shaft of the
gearbox drives a new chain segment oriented in the required plane (pins
horizontal for vertical bending, pins vertical for horizontal bending).
Each segment uses only its native bending direction, guided by rollers.
The gearboxes are the only active components at plane changes, and they
are off‑the‑shelf commodity items.

Applications

· Sliding gates. The drive motor hides in a cabinet beside the gate
opening; the chain runs horizontally through guide rollers around the
corner and connects to the gate leaf. No motor visible at the gate end.

· Industrial doors and curtains. Drive chains route around structural
columns, through wall penetrations, or into ceiling voids.

· Stage machinery. Linear actuators for set pieces route under the
stage, around obstacles, and emerge at the required lift point.

· Linear conveyance. Any application where a straight‑line chain drive
is obstructed can be re‑routed without additional motors.

Feasibility

The system uses only mature, mass‑produced components in a novel
arrangement.

· Reoriented roller chain. Standard ANSI roller chain functions
identically regardless of pin orientation. The only change is the
mounting plane of the sprockets and support structure. No custom chain
manufacturing is required.

· Support and guide rollers. Flanged U‑groove rollers (e.g., Fenner
Drives, Interroll) are produced in volume for conveyor systems and can
be mounted on simple bearing studs. For corner guide rollers, a larger
diameter reduces chain articulation stress; off‑the‑shelf nylon or steel
cam followers are suitable.

· Right‑angle drive. Bevel‑gear boxes (e.g., Boston Gear, Motovario) and
worm‑gear reducers are commodity items available in ratios from 1:1 to
60:1, with input and output shafts in any orthogonal configuration.

· Modular plane‑change gearboxes. The same right‑angle boxes used for
the motor connection serve as the plane‑change junctions. A single
gearbox type can be stocked and used at any transition point.

· Chain tensioning. A spring‑loaded or adjustable idler roller on the
return run maintains proper tension, identical to conventional chain
tensioners.

· Analogous systems. Conveyor systems routinely use horizontal chain
loops with guide rollers for directional changes (e.g., table‑top chain
in bottling lines). The innovation here is applying that same principle
to power‑transmission roller chain in linear drive applications,
combined with the modular plane‑change architecture.

Challenges

· Chain sag between supports. Longer spans require more frequent support
rollers, increasing part count. For spans exceeding 3--4 m, a continuous
support rail (UHMW polyethylene or PTFE strip) may be more economical
than discrete rollers.

· Wear on guide rollers. The chain side‑plates bear against the guide
roller surface under tension. Over time, this can wear the roller or the
chain. Hardened steel rollers and proper lubrication mitigate this.

· Lateral forces on supports. A tensioned chain pulling around a guide
roller exerts a lateral force vector that must be anchored. The roller
mounting brackets require sufficient rigidity and wall/frame attachment.

· Debris ingress in horizontal orientation. A chain lying flat can
collect dust, sand, or water in the link cavities. A simple cover or
wiper strip resolves this in outdoor applications.

· Torque capacity at plane‑change gearboxes. Each gearbox must be sized
for the full chain tension load, which may be substantial in heavy gates
or industrial doors. This adds cost at each plane‑change point compared
to the purely passive roller‑based horizontal turns.

Development Path

1\. Benchtop demonstrator. Assemble a single‑plane horizontal chain loop
with one motor, a bevel‑gear drive, two corner guide rollers, and
support rollers on a plywood table. Validate tension behavior, roller
wear, and smooth cornering.

2\. Side‑by‑side comparison with conventional gate drive. Install the
system on a sliding gate test rig alongside a standard end‑mounted chain
drive. Measure current draw, noise, wear rate, and installation time.

3\. Plane‑change prototype. Add a right‑angle gearbox to the benchtop
loop and route a secondary chain segment vertically. Verify power
transfer efficiency and alignment tolerance.

4\. Outdoor endurance test. Run a continuous cycling test on an exposed
gate installation for 100,000 cycles. Monitor roller and chain wear,
corrosion, and tension drift.

5\. Design guide and open‑source CAD release. Publish standard mounting
brackets, roller spacing tables, and gearbox sizing guidelines for
common gate and door applications.

Technology Stack

· Chain: ANSI #40 or #50 roller chain (Diamond or Tsubaki), standard
single‑strand, with master links for field assembly.

· Support rollers: 50 mm diameter U‑groove nylon rollers on 6202‑2RS
ball bearings, mounted on 12 mm steel studs at 0.5--1.5 m spacing
depending on chain tension.

· Corner guide rollers: 100 mm diameter hardened steel cam followers
(McGill CCFE series) on vertical shafts; two per corner.

· Drive bevel gearbox: Boston Gear M1150 series, 1:1 ratio, hardened
steel bevel gears, sealed and lubricated for life.

· Plane‑change gearbox: Same as drive gearbox, with input and output
flanges adaptable to chain sprockets.

· Drive motor: ½--2 hp NEMA 56C frame motor (single‑phase or
three‑phase) with integrated brake for gate applications.

· Tensioner: Spring‑loaded idler sprocket on a pivoting arm, or an
adjustable slide‑plate with lead‑screw adjustment.

· Chain cover: Extruded aluminum U‑channel with snap‑on lid, protecting
the horizontal chain run from debris and weather.
