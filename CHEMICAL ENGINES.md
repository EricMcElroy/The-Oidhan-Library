CHEMICAL ENGINES

A continuous-feed powder motor using calcium carbide and dry water, with
air and pure oxygen variants; the oxygen variant captures CO₂ as solid
limestone for near-zero emissions.

Abstract

The Chemical Engine is a solid-fuel motor that runs on dry, shelf-stable
powders: calcium carbide (CaC₂) and "dry water" (water encapsulated in
silica). Twin screw feeders meter the reactants into a hot reaction
chamber where acetylene is generated and auto-ignites. The engine is
presented in two variants. Variant A breathes air and produces a hot gas
stream for mechanical work, with a benign calcium hydroxide dust
exhaust. Variant B uses stored oxygen and exploits the co-produced
calcium hydroxide to chemically bind CO₂ into calcium carbonate (CaCO₃),
leaving an exhaust dominated by steam and a solid chalk powder that can
be spread as soil conditioner. The engine avoids liquid water,
preventing the rapid sludging and deposit formation that would otherwise
seize a continuous calcium-based reactor. All byproducts are non-toxic
solids or water vapour. The concept is disclosed here as prior art,
intended for low-cost stationary power, irrigation pumping, or light
traction.

\-\--

Problem

Internal combustion engines burn liquid hydrocarbons and emit nitrogen
oxides, soot, and uncaptured CO₂. Solid propellant motors rely on
expensive, toxic, or synthetic oxidisers. There exists no simple,
throttleable heat engine that operates entirely on low-cost dry powders,
consumes water in a solid form, and leaves a solid residue that is
environmentally harmless. Calcium carbide has been used for over a
century in acetylene lamps, but never integrated into a continuous-feed,
power-producing engine. A motor that does this---and that, in one
configuration, uses its own reaction byproduct to capture its carbon as
solid limestone---would represent a new category of clean, storable
prime mover.

Concept

The engine is built around one or more heated reaction chambers, each
fed by independent variable-speed screw augers from sealed hoppers. The
two primary hoppers contain granular calcium carbide and dry water
(silica-encapsulated water). An optional third hopper holds additional
dry water for steam dilution in the oxygen variant.

Core Reaction (all variants):

When dry water and CaC₂ meet at a temperature above \~100 °C, the silica
shells rupture, releasing water. The carbide reacts immediately:

CaC₂ + 2 H₂O → C₂H₂ (acetylene) + Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime), ΔH ≈
-127 kJ/mol.

The chamber is pre-heated by a small glow element. Once the exotherm
begins, the chamber temperature rapidly exceeds the auto-ignition
temperature of acetylene (305 °C), so no external ignition is needed.

Variant A -- Air-Breathing:

A regulated air intake admits atmospheric oxygen. Acetylene burns:
2 C₂H₂ + 5 O₂ (from air) → 4 CO₂ + 2 H₂O. The hot gas stream, carrying
fine Ca(OH)₂ dust, expands in a piston cylinder or turbine. The solid
Ca(OH)₂ is alkaline and can be collected and used for soil pH
correction.

Variant B -- Oxygen-Breathing with Carbon Capture:

Instead of air, a small pressurized oxygen cylinder feeds stoichiometric
oxygen. The oxy-acetylene flame temperature (\~3500 K) would melt any
chamber, so additional dry water is injected as a steam-generating
working fluid, absorbing the extreme heat and producing a high-pressure
mixture of steam and CO₂ at a manageable 1000--1200 K. The co-produced
Ca(OH)₂ reacts with CO₂ in a downstream capture chamber maintained at
500--750 °C: Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O. The net result is a gas stream
of mostly steam, with carbon bound as solid limestone. Any residual CO₂
slip is minimal.

Chamber Separation:

To manage the conflicting temperature and reaction
requirements---acetylene generation (fast, exothermic, solid-producing),
combustion (extreme temperature), and carbonation (moderate
temperature)---the engine can be split into distinct functional zones or
separate, linked chambers. Screw conveyors move solid intermediates
(Ca(OH)₂) from the generation zone to a carbonation zone, while gases
pass through interconnecting valves. This modular approach prevents the
carbon-capture chamber from overheating and decomposing the freshly
formed CaCO₃ back into CaO and CO₂ (which occurs above \~825 °C).

Deposit Management and Dry Water's Role:

Using liquid water would create a wet, alkaline slurry that sets into
rock-hard lime scale within minutes, seizing any continuous engine. Dry
water releases H₂O only when the silica capsules crack thermally,
keeping the reaction environment "dry" on a macroscopic level. The
Ca(OH)₂ and CaCO₃ form as free-flowing, granular powders that can be
mechanically extracted from the chamber base by a screw-extractor before
they sinter. The silica shells themselves act as a flow aid and mild
abrasive that scours surfaces and inhibits scale buildup. A rotating or
vibrating chamber interior can further discourage adhesion.

Throttle and Shutdown:

Power is modulated by feeder speed. Stopping the screws halts reactant
supply; a brief purge of air or oxygen clears residual acetylene.

Feasibility

The individual chemical and mechanical steps are well established:

· Acetylene generation is mature (miners' lamps, welding gas
generators).

· Dry water is commercially available, stable, and flowable.

· Screw feeding of powders is a precision industrial technique.

· Auto-ignition of acetylene in a hot chamber is reliable.

· CO₂ capture by lime is practised in flue-gas scrubbing and can be
rapid in the correct temperature window.

· High-temperature materials: ceramic-lined chambers (silicon carbide,
alumina) can withstand operating conditions. Steam dilution in Variant B
brings temperatures into the service range of nickel superalloys or
advanced ceramics.

· Solid extraction screws and rotary valves handle hot cement and ash in
other industries.

Integration risk is moderate but solvable with a modular chamber
approach.

Challenges

1\. Calcium and lime deposits: Even with dry water, sublimation,
condensation, and local hotspots can cause Ca(OH)₂ or CaCO₃ scale to
adhere. Chamber geometry that avoids dead zones, active scraping
mechanisms, and the scouring action of silica particles are first-line
mitigations. Separating the generation, combustion, and carbonation
chambers prevents wet sludge from forming in the combustion zone.

2\. CaCO₃ decomposition: In Variant B, if the carbonation chamber
exceeds \~825 °C, limestone decomposes back to CaO + CO₂, undoing the
capture. Active temperature control---by metering the steam diluent and
separating the hot combustion gas from the carbonation bed until it has
cooled---is required.

3\. Silica shell accumulation: The silica particles persist as a fine,
hard dust that can erode valves, piston rings, and turbine blades.
Cyclonic filtration and regular maintenance intervals can handle it, but
wear life must be empirically established.

4\. Acetylene safety: Acetylene is explosive over a wide range in air
(2.5--81%). The engine must maintain a fuel-rich atmosphere in the
generation zone and lean conditions only after deliberate mixing. Flame
arrestors and rapid purge valves are mandatory.

5\. Particle flow consistency: Calcium carbide is hygroscopic; dry water
can clump if storage is compromised. Hermetic hoppers, desiccant
breathers, and vibratory agitators on the feeders ensure reliable
metering.

6\. Steam-induced hydrolysis: In Variant B, superheated steam in contact
with CaCO₃ can reverse the carbonation reaction. Maintaining the
carbonation zone below 600 °C and minimising steam exposure time
mitigates this.

Development Path

1\. Phase 1 -- Batch reaction tests: Manually meter CaC₂ and dry water
in a sealed, instrumented vessel. Confirm gas yields, solid composition,
and the absence of cementitious scale when dry water is used. Test steam
dilution ratios for Variant B.

2\. Phase 2 -- Continuous feeder rig: Build a single-chamber prototype
with two screw feeders. Operate Variant A (air) with a small piston
engine cylinder as the expander. Measure power, solid output, and
deposition rate.

3\. Phase 3 -- Modular chamber proof: Construct a three-zone prototype
(generator, combustion, carbonation) for Variant B. Use an oxygen bottle
and a separate carbonation vessel with screw extractor. Quantify CO₂
capture efficiency and solid output purity.

4\. Phase 4 -- Durability test stand: Run the engine continuously for
50+ hours, monitoring erosion, deposit thickness, and seal integrity.
Evaluate ceramic coatings and scraper designs.

5\. Phase 5 -- Open release: Publish engineering drawings, material
specifications, and operational data. The Chemical Engine becomes a
public-domain system suitable for low-cost, off-grid power.

Technology Stack

Component Example Tech

Fuel hopper Sealed stainless steel, desiccant breather, 5 kg capacity,
vibratory pad

Dry water hopper Food-grade hopper, 10 kg capacity, with anti-bridging
agitator

Screw feeders 15 mm diameter hardened steel augers, NEMA 23 steppers,
0.1--5 g/s feed rate

Reaction chamber (gen) Alumina ceramic tube, 500--700 °C operating, with
integrated glow-plug starter

Combustion chamber (Var. B) Silicon carbide tube, with tangential oxygen
and steam injectors

Carbonation chamber (Var. B) Agitated bed vessel, temperature-controlled
550--650 °C, solid extraction screw

Air intake (Var. A) Throttle body with MAF sensor and flame arrestor

Oxygen supply (Var. B) Medical-grade O₂ cylinder, pressure regulator,
mass flow controller

Power extraction Piston: modified small diesel engine block; Turbine:
Capstone C30 microturbine with ceramic inlet

Solid extraction Water-cooled screw conveyor, collecting CaCO₃/silica
powder in a sealed drum

Control system PLC (Siemens LOGO! or Arduino Mega) with thermocouple and
pressure sensors, safety relays

Author Note

This is a paper about a motor that runs on powdered rock and dry water,
and makes chalk instead of smog. I haven't built it. No prototype
exists, and I'm not claiming a workshop success story. What's here is
the chemical pathway, the mechanical logic, and the workarounds for the
obvious ways it tries to clog itself with lime. The components are
off-the-shelf; the engineering is hammer-and-tune, not aerospace. The
blueprint is public. If someone with a hopper, a kiln, and a healthy
disregard for conventional engines wants to give it a go, the math says
it'll work.
