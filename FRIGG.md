## Abstract

Paracord Structures is a material fabrication system that transforms
flexible cordage into rigid, high-strength load-bearing forms using
composite matrix mechanics. The system pairs Type III 550 nylon paracord
with low-viscosity epoxy resin, exploiting capillary wicking,
interfacial bonding, and controlled polymerization to create
self-supporting structural elements without internal metal or wood
framing. The resulting material locks tensile fibers in fixed spatial
constraints, yielding free-standing objects with polished, translucent
aesthetics and structural properties competitive with timber. No
specialized machining or high-energy processing is required. Builders
can mold, weave, or suspend resin-saturated cordage into functional
geometries ranging from furniture to outdoor fixtures.

## Problem

Structural fabrication relies on rigid mediums---timber, milled metal,
molded plastic---that require subtractive machining, high-energy
additive processing, and specialized tooling. These materials offer
limited aesthetic variety and constrain geometric freedom. At the
opposite extreme, textile and cordage applications are confined to
flexible, high-tensile roles such as tying and lifting. When rope or
paracord is woven into functional shapes like chairs, it remains
dependent on an external rigid frame to hold its form. No accessible,
low-overhead method allows flexible fiber lines to serve as their own
self-supporting skeleton.

## Concept

The system establishes a composite paradigm combining flexible,
high-tensile synthetic cordage with an enveloping polymer matrix.

**Core Composite Pairing:** Type III 550 paracord (nylon) serves as
primary reinforcing fiber. Low-viscosity epoxy resin serves as
stabilizing matrix. The nylon exterior sheath exhibits high surface
energy and specific texture, forming an intense molecular bond with
liquid epoxy during cross-linking.

**Capillary Wicking Action:** Liquid resin applied to the cordage is
drawn into the woven sheath via capillary action, saturating internal
strands before polymerization locks the structure.

**Forming and Geometry Manipulation:** Prior to curing, the saturated
paracord is woven, wrapped around removable mandrels, or tensioned into
geometric frames. The material behaves flexibly during layout, enabling
organic curves and intricate weaves impossible with standard rigid
stocks.

**Polymerization and Free-Standing Rigidity:** As the epoxy cures into a
cross-linked solid, it locks the high-tensile nylon fibers under fixed
spatial constraints. The resulting composite requires no internal
skeleton to bear weight.

**Aesthetic Translucency:** Paracord\'s inherent sheen and wide color
variety, combined with clear or tinted epoxy, produces a polished,
translucent finish where internal woven patterns remain visible beneath
a glass-like exterior.

## Feasibility

**Fiber-Reinforced Polymer (FRP) Mechanics:** The engineering principles
are identical to industrial fiberglass and carbon fiber composites.
Internal fibers provide tensile strength while the cured resin provides
compressive strength and structural alignment. Type III 550 paracord has
a rated breaking strength of 550 pounds, providing exceptional internal
tensile reinforcement.

**Interfacial Bonding Energy:** Nylon contains polar amide groups along
its backbone that exhibit strong hydrogen bonding and chemical affinity
with epoxide groups in uncured resin. This high interfacial adhesion
ensures smooth load transfer across the fiber-matrix boundary,
preventing delamination under stress.

**Weight-to-Strength Parity:** The composite is heavier than softwoods
but exceeds wood in density, impact resistance, and shear thresholds. It
functions as a close alternative to metal or timber framing for
structural and artistic assemblies.

**Precedent:** Industrial filament winding, Micarta fabrication, and FRP
composite manufacturing all validate the core mechanism. The materials
are off-the-shelf commodities. No custom chemistry or proprietary
hardware is required.

## Challenges

**Exothermic Heat Management:** Epoxy cures exothermically. Thick
bundles of saturated cordage can trap heat, causing thermal spikes that
smoke, crack, yellow, or melt the nylon. Mitigation requires
slow-curing, deep-pour resin formulations and staged layering.

**Incomplete Core Saturation:** Paracord\'s outer sheath surrounds seven
to nine loose inner nylon strands. Dense resins may not penetrate fully
through wicking alone. Maximum strength requires low-viscosity resins or
mechanical compression rollers during wetting.

**Air Bubble Entrapment:** Woven cordage traps micro-pockets of air.
These form structural voids in the cured composite, reducing compressive
strength. Vacuum degassing, localized heat, or vibrating tables during
molding address the issue.

## Development Path

**Phase 1 -- Material Sample and Tensile Testing:** Saturate short
lengths of various paracord brands and colors in clear epoxy. Cure into
rigid rods. Perform manual bend, fracture, and clarity tests to identify
optimal weave and finish combinations.

**Phase 2 -- Joint and Linkage Engineering:** Develop methods for
joining cured elements or experiment with continuous weaving techniques
where a single unbroken cord traces the entire structural boundary. Test
knot strength when locked by cured resin.

**Phase 3 -- Functional Prototype (Lawn Chair Baseline):** Design and
build a full-scale chair or stool using a removable temporary jig. Weave
resin-saturated paracord over the formwork into a self-supporting grid
pattern. Validate weight-bearing capacity after curing and jig removal.

**Phase 4 -- Alternative Matrix Mapping:** Test lower-cost curing agents
such as specialized wood glues or acrylic polymers on alternative
natural cords, establishing a matrix library for interior decorative and
lower-load artistic applications.

## Why Now

Structural epoxies, deep-pour resins, and tactical nylon cordage have
transitioned from specialized industrial supplies to low-cost
commodities. The modern maker space converges functional industrial
design with accessible DIY fabrication. A material historically reserved
for survival kits, when tied to composite science, allows builders to
bypass traditional carpentry and metalworking tools---using hands,
simple molds, and liquid chemistry to create permanent structures with
unique aesthetic depth.

## Author Note

This project occupies the intersection of structural utility and visual
style. Metal and wood will always win on pure weight-to-stability
optimization. But paracord composite is close enough to be fully
functional, and it produces surfaces and geometries that rigid stocks
cannot. A single spool of colored cord, saturated in clear polymer, can
be knitted or wrapped into any shape and cured into a glass-smooth piece
of furniture that lives outdoors indefinitely without rotting, rusting,
or breaking.
