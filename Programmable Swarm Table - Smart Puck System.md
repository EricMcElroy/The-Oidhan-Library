\## Abstract

The Programmable Swarm Table / Smart Puck System defines a modular
physical manipulation architecture that treats physical objects as
dynamic, programmable pixels within a workspace. By centralizing power
delivery, high-resolution tracking, processing, and absolute mechanical
stabilization within a stationary intelligent table surface, the
individual mobile puck units are kept fundamentally simple, inexpensive,
and highly scalable. The system coordinates these autonomous units
across a precision reference plane to rapidly deploy reconfigurable
manufacturing jigs, interactive simulation environments, and adaptive
physical fixtures.

\## Problem Statement

Traditional automation, robotic material handling, and manufacturing
fixture arrays rely on high-complexity, single-purpose machinery such as
multi-axis robotic arms or custom CNC-machined tooling plates. These
systems are inherently rigid, capital-intensive, and prone to
single-point failures; if a dedicated robotic assembly fails, the entire
workspace goes offline. Conversely, existing mobile swarm robotics
systems shift the hardware burden onto the individual agents, requiring
complex onboard power storage, computational logic, and expensive
spatial sensor payloads that make deploying them in large numbers
cost-prohibitive.

\## Concept Overview

This system fundamentally reverses the traditional design paradigm by
shifting complexity away from the mobile agents and integrating it
directly into the workspace infrastructure. The interaction loop is
governed by a precise dual-state operating architecture:

1\. \*\*Movement Mode:\*\* The table disables its localized
electromagnetic clamping arrays and energizes a continuous wireless
surface power field. The individual pucks draw energy directly from this
field to actuate their internal locomotion systems, moving dynamically
across the surface. A centralized tracking and compute layer monitors
the global state, calculating collision-free trajectories and feeding
precise coordinate updates to the swarm.

2\. \*\*Locking Mode:\*\* Once the pucks navigate to their exact
calculated destinations, the table selectively activates localized
electromagnets beneath each unit. This engages the pucks\' ferromagnetic
bases, locking them into a rigid, non-yielding physical grid.

Once secured, the entire cluster transitions from a fluid swarm into an
unyielding mechanical structure capable of serving as a heavy-duty
assembly jig, optical test bench, or high-stability physical reference
frame.

\## Why This Works --- Feasibility Basis

The feasibility of this system is rooted in the decoupling of kinetic
transit from static structural resistance. By using localized magnetic
field capture, we can transform low-power mobile units into
exceptionally rigid structural pillars.

\## Known Engineering Challenges

Building a seamless, high-density swarm surface introduces several
unique engineering hurdles:

\* \*\*Thermal Management of Low-Latency Coils:\*\* Maintaining an array
of high-density electromagnets in an active, prolonged \"Locking Mode\"
generates significant resistive heat (I\^2R losses). Without embedded
liquid cooling channels or advanced heat sinking within the table
substrate, thermal expansion could warp the precision reference surface.

\* \*\*Inductive Cross-Talk and Field Uniformity:\*\* Simultaneously
broadcasting surface-wide wireless power while maintaining tight,
discrete spatial control over localized locking electromagnets creates
significant electromagnetic interference (EMI). This requires advanced
high-frequency shielding between the power transmission coils and the
static retention arrays.

\* \*\*Locomotion Slip and Traction Anomalies:\*\* Because the table
must maintain a low-friction, easy-to-clean precision reference plane,
the pucks must operate on a smooth surface. This inherently limits
mechanical traction, making the units susceptible to slipping if dust,
oils, or manufacturing debris contaminate the plane.

\* \*\*Surface-to-Agent Communication Latency:\*\* As the number of
active pucks scales from dozens to hundreds, routing real-time
collision-avoidance telemetry requires a high-bandwidth,
ultra-low-latency local wireless mesh network or a highly modulated
near-field data transmission layer embedded directly within the table
surface.

\## Suggested Development Path

1\. \*\*Phase 1: Small-Scale Bench Prototype:\*\* Construct a 12x12 inch
fixed matrix using standard open-source PCB coils for wireless power
transmission and discrete, off-the-shelf electromagnets. Build three
pucks utilizing small, capacitor-buffered vibration or micro-wheel
drivetrains to validate basic state transitions between free movement
and rigid lock-up.

2\. \*\*Phase 2: Closed-Loop Vision Integration:\*\* Mount a high-speed
overhead global shutter camera above the table workspace. Program a
centralized control node to trace fiducial markers on the pucks\' top
surfaces, feeding real-time positional error corrections to the swarm to
automate paths and eliminate cumulative step-drift.

3\. \*\*Phase 3: Modular Mosaic Scaling:\*\* Design the table as a
tileable, modular interlocking system. Each tile should contain its own
independent microcontroller, power regulation circuit, and localized
sensor grid, allowing multiple table blocks to snap together to create
expansive, infinitely reconfigurable smart production floors.

\## Why Now

The realization of the Smart Puck System is made viable by the
convergence of low-cost, surface-mount PCB fabrication techniques and
the mass adoption of consumer wireless charging standards (such as Qi2
and high-frequency resonant inductive coupling). Additionally, the
dramatic reduction in the cost of high-density micro-electromagnet
arrays---coupled with the availability of ultra-cheap, highly integrated
microcontrollers (like the ESP32 or RP2040 architecture)---allows for
the cost-effective production of hundreds of synchronized mobile units
without compounding hardware expenses.

\## Author Note

By stripping the battery mass, heavy processing chips, and complex
encoders out of the mobile agents, we redefine the economics of swarm
robotics. The pucks become highly disposable, interchangeable, and
easily maintained assets, while the environment itself handles the heavy
lifting of spatial tracking and structural rigidity. It is an
architecture built to turn static workspaces into living, adaptive
infrastructure.
