ELECTRIC GOLF CARTS

A modular, narrow-track electric vehicle platform with hot-swappable
bodies, tool attachments, and batteries---serving as a neighborhood
runabout, a job site power hub, and a scalable farm utility train.

Abstract

The Electric Golf Cart system is a compact, 44-inch-wide electric
vehicle built on a standardized rolling chassis that accepts
interchangeable body modules, tool attachments, and hot-swappable
battery packs. In its lightest configuration it is a two-seat,
open-sided neighborhood cruiser governed to 25 mph and narrow enough to
slip through a residential swing gate. The same chassis, with no
mechanical changes, can be reconfigured into a four-seat people mover, a
micro cargo truck, a mobile job site power station running heavy
electric tools, or---by scaling the footprint and adding
articulation---a farm utility train with all-wheel steering. A low-cost
licensing model invites accessory manufacturers to build compatible
modules while maintaining quality control. The system turns a single
vehicle purchase into a multi-purpose power tool, reducing the need for
separate cars, generators, and single-task engine-powered equipment.

\-\--

Problem

Households and small operations accumulate a fleet of single-purpose
machines: a car for errands, a golf cart for the neighborhood, a
pressure washer, a generator, a log splitter, a concrete mixer---each
with its own engine, battery, or fuel system, each sitting idle 95% of
the time. They are expensive, they require storage, and they demand
individual maintenance. Meanwhile, light electric vehicles (NEVs, golf
carts) have remained largely single-configuration, offering no
modularity beyond cosmetic accessories. There is no platform that lets
an owner start with a simple neighborhood runabout and expand the same
machine into a construction tool, a mobile power station, or a farm
vehicle, using a common power bus and mechanical interface.

Concept

The system is built around a compact, flat rolling chassis containing
the drive-by-wire controls, steering, braking, and a small onboard
battery. Everything above and beyond that is modular.

Core Dimensions and Constraints (Neighborhood Base):

· Width: 44 inches (fits through standard 48-inch swing gates).

· No enclosed doors; open sides allow shoulders to extend slightly
beyond the body line for narrow passages.

· Length varies by body configuration: approximately 8 feet for a
two-seater, 10 feet for a four-seater or two-seater with a short cargo
deck.

· Top speed governed to 25 mph (NEV class) for on-road use in zones up
to 35 mph; limiter removable for private land.

Power Architecture:

· Base vehicle carries a small integrated battery providing roughly 30
minutes of drive time, keeping initial cost and weight low.

· A standardized battery bay accepts hot-swappable packs: slide out the
small pack, slide in a larger one, or stack multiple packs for extended
runtime.

· A hybrid gas generator module can be inserted into the same bay,
charging the onboard battery and supplying sustained high-current power
for job site attachments. All modules share a common DC power and data
bus routed to standardized connector points along the chassis rails.

Body and Seating Modules:

· Occupied seats mount to reinforced hard points with bolted
connections, meeting safety requirements.

· Cargo decks, rear bench seats, solar roof panels, and storage boxes
attach via a combination of hard mounts and quick-release latch rails.

· Configurations: two-seat runabout, two-seat with cargo deck, four-seat
passenger, two-seat with extended flatbed, and more.

Accessory Ecosystem:

A universal accessory rail runs along the chassis, carrying power and a
CAN data bus. Modules click in and identify themselves automatically.
Two broad classes exist:

1\. Neighborhood & Lifestyle: Bluetooth speaker bar, portable projector
with fold-out screen, LED ambient lighting, tailgate seating, insulated
cooler box with active cooling, secure package locker, high-sided yard
waste panels, tool caddy, weather canopy, heated/cooled seat elements,
pet kennel, mobility ramp, neighborhood watch camera kit, emergency
first-aid/fire module, little free library, water dispenser, planter
bed, bike repair stand, and more.

2\. Job Site Edition: Air compressor, pressure washer, electric log
splitter, small concrete/mortar mixer drum, post hole auger with
articulating arm, water transfer pump, inverter module (cart becomes a
backup generator), and the hybrid gas generator module itself. These are
motor- or pump-based tools that bolt to the chassis hard points and plug
into the power bus, turning the cart into a mobile job site power
station.

Farm Extension:

For agricultural use, a scaled-up chassis variant (wider, longer
wheelbase, higher ground clearance) shares the same modular philosophy.
Speed limiter removed for private land. Knobby tires replace street
treads. An articulating powered trailer hitch allows additional
steerable, driven trailers to be linked, forming an all-wheel-steer
electric road train controlled by an AI path-tracking system. All
wheels, including on the main chassis, can achieve high steering angles
(up to 90° for crab maneuvers). Attachments include bale spears,
sprayers, seeders, and other implements.

Licensing:

Chassis attachment points, rail dimensions, power bus protocols, and
communication standards are published under a low-cost,
quality-controlled license. Accessory manufacturers can design and sell
compatible modules without heavy upfront fees, incentivizing a broad
ecosystem while maintaining safety and interoperability standards.

Feasibility

· Electric NEV platforms are proven technology; manufacturers such as
Club Car, E-Z-GO, and Polaris already produce street-legal low-speed
vehicles with lithium battery options.

· Hot-swappable battery systems are used in construction equipment
(e.g., Milwaukee MX Fuel, Green Machine) and electric outboards.

· CAN bus power/data distribution is a mature automotive standard;
specifying a common connector and communication protocol is
straightforward.

· Quick-release mechanical interfaces (L-track, locking cleats, toggle
clamps) are widely used in cargo, aviation, and film industries for
rapidly securing payloads.

· Hub motor technology enables independent wheel control without axles,
facilitating all-wheel steering in the farm variant.

· AI path-tracking for multi-unit road trains borrows from automated
guided vehicle (AGV) control and agricultural row-following systems.

Challenges

1\. Seat safety: Quick-swap seats must reliably lock into hard points
and withstand crash loads, even at low speed. A standardized, tested
locking mechanism with visual confirmation is required.

2\. Thermal management on job site tools: Sustained high-current draws
(log splitter, compressor) will stress battery packs and connectors.
Active cooling in larger battery modules and thermal cutoffs are
necessary.

3\. Weight distribution: Heavy attachments on the rear deck can unload
the front wheels, affecting steering and braking. The chassis must place
the battery mass low and forward, and heavy tool modules should sit over
or ahead of the rear axle.

4\. Articulated train dynamics: Farm road trains must prevent
jackknifing and limit joint angles to avoid tire contact. AI control
must be robust to sensor noise, mud, and uneven terrain.

5\. Regulatory variance: NEV regulations differ by jurisdiction. The
base platform must be adaptable to local lighting, mirror, and restraint
requirements.

6\. Ecosystem bootstrapping: An accessory ecosystem requires initial
adoption. A limited initial lineup of first-party modules, combined with
cheap licensing and published standards, can attract third-party
manufacturers.

Development Path

1\. Phase 1 -- Base chassis prototype: Build a single 44-inch-wide,
two-seat rolling chassis with hub motors, steering, braking, and a
simple power bus. Validate gate-clearance, handling, and battery
hot-swap.

2\. Phase 2 -- Body module design: Fabricate and test the hard-point
seat mounts, cargo deck latch system, and accessory rail. Demonstrate a
full reconfiguration between two-seat, four-seat, and cargo deck in
under 30 minutes.

3\. Phase 3 -- Job site attachment integration: Develop and test the air
compressor, pressure washer, and generator/inverter modules. Measure
power draw, runtime, and thermal behavior.

4\. Phase 4 -- Farm chassis variant: Stretch the chassis, add all-wheel
steering, and build a single powered articulating trailer. Test
path-following AI on varied terrain.

5\. Phase 5 -- Licensing and ecosystem launch: Publish the interface
standard, offer a low-cost license, and release open specifications for
the power/data bus. Ship a reference accessory kit to incentivize
third-party development.

Technology Stack

Component Example Tech

Hub Motors Direct-drive brushless DC hub motors (e.g., QS Motor 3000W),
independent wheel control

Steering Steer-by-wire with brushless servo actuators, 90° capable (farm
variant) or 60° (neighborhood)

Battery Architecture 48V modular LiFePO₄ packs with slide-lock connector
and CAN BMS; optional series/parallel stacking

Power/Data Bus 48V DC bus, CAN 2.0B, weather-sealed 4-pin + 2-pin data
connectors at standard mounting points

Accessory Rail Extruded aluminum T-slot or L-track with integrated bus
bar; quick-release toggle clamps

Seat Hard Points Bolted steel receivers in chassis frame with
spring-loaded locking pins and visual lock indicators

Vehicle Controller Open-source VCU (e.g., Pixhawk derivative) with ROS2
for farm coordination; CAN gateway for modules

Hybrid Generator Module Small 4-stroke engine coupled to a 48V
alternator, regulated output, auto-start based on SOC

AI Path-Tracking (Farm) Stereo cameras and RTK GPS; custom
path-following controller coordinating lead and trailer steer angles

Body Panels Recycled HDPE or painted aluminum; modular panels attach via
the rail system

Author Note

I don't own a factory. I haven't bent a single tube of a chassis. This
white paper is a design for a system that could replace a garage full of
engines with one quiet, narrow cart that transforms into whatever you
need that day. The battery swap concept, the attachment rail, the cheap
license for third-party makers---it's all laid out here as prior art. If
a manufacturer picks it up and builds an ecosystem where a neighborhood
runabout also mixes concrete and powers a fridge during a blackout, the
idea worked. The blueprint is public. Build it, share it, and send
photos.
